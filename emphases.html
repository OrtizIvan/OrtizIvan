Earth Sciences

Geology, geography, hydrology, biology, climate science, soil science - in English one speaks of all these research areas when one speaks of Earth Sciences. In German, this umbrella term does not really exist. You can't be an expert in all these areas. Usually you are in one and then learn from the others. Geology, (paleo)climatology, the recognition of landscape structures, the application of geographic information systems, resource management, aerosols, climate change, glacial and interglacial were primarily the focal points of my bachelor's degree, but, like e.B climatology and atmospheric sciences, also part of my master's degree. How do global ocean and wind currents work, which soil types are particularly productive (loess and black earths) and where can they be found? Why does granite hardly weather in Europe, but very quickly in the tropics? Which minerals are common and what are they indicators for? What are karst landscapes, what are stromatolites and ooids? This is classical geology, important to understand the basics of the climate system. For our lives today, however, questions about the influence of greenhouse gases and their formation are more important. Or considering how to build cities to regulate hot summer temperatures. Knowing ideas and methods to reduce water demand and traffic and thus make cities more livable, for example, is a central area of application for transdisciplinary thinking and acting.

Hydrology and water management

Seen from space, the world appears blue. The blue planet! It is home to almost 1,400 billion kmof water. But of all these magnificent water reserves on Earth, only between 35 and 45 billion km are3 fresh water! And not even this is completely available to us, because it is bound to more 2/3 in glaciers and ice sheets, while in the groundwater there are another 30%. So water is a precious commodity. And the future looks rather bleak. We pump empty underground aquifers that have filled up over millennia to millions of years. In many places, we pollute our surface waters with plastic, residues from industry and dirty water mixed with human residues. But water is not only precious. It is often enough also a threat. Too much water during storms, floods or floods presents us with great challenges. Understanding how water is distributed on Earth, through which processes it is transported, how to use it (sparingly) and what options there are to purify it are some of the tasks of hydrology. The basics can be developed with calculations such as .B flow rate or hydraulics. For groundwater and flow behavior, some geological understanding is also needed to determine e.B. the catchment area and the flow direction. And for disposal and cleaning, it then needs chemical understandings. These are also necessary to achieve the ecotoxicological effects...

Biology and Ecotoxicology

Ecotoxicology,, i.e. the doctrine of environmental toxins, was the background of my thesis. Pesticides on fields and in the water, plastic and microplastics in nature. Drug residues. Xenobiotics are everywhere. They have their benefits, e.B. as pesticides for a more reliable agricultural production method, but in nature they cause harm. They are bioaccumulative in living beings, persistent and toxic, preferably accumulate locally in the soil and waters, contribute to genetic damage and possibly species extinction. Through combustion processes, they enter the atmosphere, influence climate change and are also dangerous for humans. Through my thesis and modules such as ecotoxicology and aquatic ecology, I have learned to identify input pathways, understand guidelines, determine chemicals and apply possible (unfortunately it is not always possible) measures to remove xenobiotics from the environment.
